# The Technical Crossroads

Today I asked my senior lab mates: "I feel stuck at a crossroads — one step back is device interaction, one step forward is artificial intelligence. How did you figure out which of the thousands of papers you actually wanted to study?"

They said:

Actually, what you're calling device interaction and AI + design are just tools.
You shouldn't get lost in the tools right now. The real question is: what is your research question? What problem do you actually want to solve?
Device interaction and AI + design aren't two separate paths — they're tools you can use together.




# The Problem You Want to Solve Is What Matters

The most important point is still: what problem do you want to solve?

When we read the literature,
we're not asking which technology we should use for our research.
We're asking: which problems keep coming up? Which problems haven't been solved well? Which problems do you actually care about?

So the problem you want to solve matters deeply — it has to resonate with people.
Right now, technology isn't the issue; the issue is whether your problem can be understood.

Following that logic, does it seem like startups and innovative products all follow the same pattern?
That evening at the izakaya, listening to the professor talk about innovation in architectural academia — maybe that was just my old way of thinking.
But after talking with you all today,
perhaps it really is: problem framing >>> solution.

Because the solution can always be faked (the technology just looks impressive)? As long as something gets built, that counts.
Like Annie's thesis: Multi-modal Interaction => IoT.
But reading it at the time, if you didn't know the technology, you'd think it was some advanced approach.
The point is always to accomplish things simply.
Complexity is just showing off.

## If the Problem Isn't Addressed, It's Useless

> Take my research on cross-domain communication — why that topic? Because I studied Information Management in undergrad and came here to study design. I always found it hard to understand what my IS classmates were saying compared to design students. At first I didn't know why. Later I started looking through the literature on cross-domain communication, and only then did I learn that it's because professional terminology differs and the knowledge backgrounds are different. Then I saw some people solving it with devices, some with AI, some with design processes. I didn't know what solution I wanted to use at the start. It just happened that the year I enrolled, AI image generation was blowing up, so my advisor told me to try it out. I submitted to a conference and got accepted. They found my problem important and common, but they were curious how I solved it — so they let me in first and had me explain in my presentation, haha. And I've just kept doing it ever since, haha.

> Honestly, after doing it this long, is cross-domain communication something I even care about? Maybe not, really. I just felt like a lot of people were working on it, and it's still a problem you run into today, and there are many ways to approach it, so I kept going. But now I'm tired of it and want to move on — except I can't drop it 🤣
